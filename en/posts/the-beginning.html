The Beginning of a Career

2026-02-06

In the second semester of my sophomore year, I seriously considered dropping out.

At the time, my wife and I were in a long-distance relationship — she was in another city. I was learning frontend development every day, but couldn't find my footing. I didn't know what I was doing, nor what college could really offer me. One night, it suddenly clicked — I'd just quit, move to her city, find a job paying 3,000 RMB, and we'd be together. The moment this thought appeared, I actually felt relieved.

I really did go for an interview. A small company — I've already forgotten what they did. The interviewer asked a few questions, and I stumbled through my answers. A few days later, I got the rejection. I sat on my dorm bed, staring at my phone screen for a long time.

I couldn't even land a job paying 3,000 RMB.

For several nights after that, I lay in bed unable to sleep, my mind filled with a jumble of thoughts. I didn't know which direction to go, didn't even know what skills I had.

Until one day, I posted a help-seeking thread on Zhihu. Most replies were generic platitudes, but one was different. That person said the real beauty of computer science isn't in frontend — it's in backend. He recommended a few books and directions. I can't remember his username anymore, but I read that reply over and over again.

After that, I set aside frontend and started learning Java. Every day I camped out in the library, typing code along with the books. I thought this was the way out.

But it was just so-so.

The real turning point came by chance. One day, while browsing the internet, I stumbled upon a company called PingCAP that was building a database called TiDB. Before that, I had never heard of distributed systems, nor did I know that databases could be built from scratch. These concepts were so far from my world — like things from another universe.

But their technical blog drew me in. Those articles were really well-written, explaining complex concepts with clarity. I read them one by one, into the early morning hours, until my eyes ached. That feeling is hard to describe — like walking in darkness for so long, and suddenly someone points you in a direction and tells you there's light over there.

I started learning like crazy. Operating systems, networking, databases — I read everything. For the entire second half of college, I was mostly on my own. Classmates said I was aloof, but really I just had no time to socialize. Every day was just classes, reading, coding, then sleep.

In my junior year, I did a two-month internship at Alibaba.

They gave me a machine learning problem — time series prediction. I knew nothing about the field, so I had to learn on the fly — reading papers, tuning parameters, running models. In the end, I got 95% accuracy.

At the time, I thought I was pretty impressive.

It took me many years to realize that 95% meant nothing. After working at Meta, I learned the concept of baseline metrics — those numbers you can achieve without any extra effort. A good engineer should demonstrate the value they create above the baseline. Back then, I didn't even know what a baseline was.

I didn't get a return offer from that internship.

Campus recruitment season started, and I sent out many resumes. Rejected by all the big companies, rejected by mid-tier ones too, barely getting a few interviews from small companies. I remember walking down the street after one interview, watching people passing by, suddenly feeling like I didn't belong in this world.

I blamed everything on my academic background.

On the first day of my Alibaba internship, nearly a hundred people sat in the conference room. I quietly looked around — almost all of them were graduate students from top-tier universities. I later found out I was the only one in that batch of interns from a second-tier undergraduate program.

During that period, I kept thinking — if I were also a graduate student, would everything be different? This thought dug deeper and deeper, eventually becoming an obsession — take the graduate entrance exam, that's the only way out.

When people are in pain, they cling to any glimmer of hope in sight and treat it as everything.

But I calmed down. Quitting my job to study full-time for the exam wasn't realistic for me — my family's situation wouldn't allow it, and I didn't have the confidence for it either. After much deliberation, I decided to find a job first and study while working.

In June 2018, I graduated. I took a development job at a very small company, earning 8,000 RMB per month. That was the best result within my capabilities.

During that time, I worked during the day and read at night. "UNIX Network Programming," "Modern Operating Systems," "Database System Concepts," "The Art of Java Concurrency Programming"... I read everything. Every night until 11 or 12, no rest on weekends either. I didn't know when any of this would be useful, but it was all I could do.

I thought I'd stay at that company for a long time.

Near the end of the year, I was browsing V2EX as usual and saw an ad. A recruiter from Singapore, looking for backend engineers, for a company called Shopee. I hesitated for a moment, then submitted my resume.

I didn't expect to actually get an interview invitation.

The interviewer seemed to be a Principal at Shopee. I can't quite remember exactly what we discussed, only that he asked very detailed questions, drilling down layer by layer from projects to fundamentals. My palms were sweating from nervousness, but I managed to share, bit by bit, what I'd read in those late-night study sessions and the notes I'd taken.

The entire interview process lasted one or two months, from phone screens to on-site interviews, round after round. The waiting days were agonizing — every time my phone buzzed, my heart would skip a beat.

The offer was finally confirmed several months later.

That evening, I sat in my rented apartment, staring at the email on my phone for a long time. Outside the window was the city's nightscape, no different from any other night. But I knew something had changed.

Looking back now, none of those detours were wasted. Seneca once said, "Luck is what happens when preparation meets opportunity." Those late nights on Zhihu, those early mornings reading TiDB blogs, those weekends studying alone — they were all preparation, waiting for an opportunity.

I've always been grateful to that interviewer. Because of that interview, I finally got on the right track. What happened next, I'll write about in the next post.

If you're like I was back then, still spinning in circles of confusion, here's what I want to say: keep making progress — it will pay off. When the opportunity comes, you need to be ready to catch it.

We just need to be patient.
