The Reality Behind the Big Tech Halo

2026-02-07

After joining Meta, I worked on product development. Full-stack — writing both frontend and backend.

The overall experience was very different from Shopee. At Shopee, I was on the infrastructure team, dealing with systems, code, and documentation every day. But at Meta, product development requires constant synchronization with business teams, proactively driving them forward, and coordinating various resources. Some people are great at this kind of work, but I'm not that type.

After a few years here, I want to talk about some real experiences behind the big tech halo.

Not complaining — just documenting.

First, the processes.

Meta's processes are indeed very well-established. Code reviews, design reviews, release processes, oncall mechanisms... every aspect has clear standards. When I first joined, I thought these were great — they ensure engineering quality.

But stay long enough and you'll find that processes inevitably spawn corresponding strategies.

There's an old Chinese saying: where there are policies from above, there are countermeasures from below. This applies to any large company. Processes are rigid, but people are adaptable. When processes become a burden, people naturally find ways to work around them or satisfy them with minimal effort.

This isn't anyone's fault — it's a systemic inevitability.

Then there's the people.

Before joining Meta, I assumed everyone here would be top-tier. After all, the interviews are so difficult — anyone who gets in must be exceptional, right?

After joining, I found the quality varies widely.

There are indeed incredibly talented people — the kind who can see the essence of a problem at a glance and write code that's both fast and excellent. Working with them, you realize how much you still have to learn.

But there are also people who coast along. Some are great at packaging themselves, great at showing up at the right place at the right time, but their actual output is limited. These people exist at every company — big tech is no exception.

The halo is the halo. People are still people.

The third observation is about performance reviews.

Meta's performance review process is very rigorous and very transparent. Every six months, your performance, your impact, your growth — all are evaluated. This mechanism has its benefits — it forces you to think about what you're doing, how well you're doing it, and whether it has value.

I used to never plan ahead. But after these years of adjustment, I've now developed the habit of planning. That's a real, tangible takeaway.

But the flip side of the coin is pressure.

When performance reviews become extremely important, many people become very utilitarian. If something doesn't have impact, they won't bother with you. If something doesn't help their performance rating, they'll deflect. When you ask someone for help, their first question is often: what's in it for me?

This isn't an individual problem — it's behavior shaped by incentive structures. When a system rewards certain behavior, people naturally gravitate toward it.

Working for the sake of performance reviews is a common survival strategy in big tech.

Finally, let's talk about technology.

Meta's tech stack is almost entirely proprietary. The frameworks, the tools, the infrastructure — all built in-house. When you first start, the learning curve is steep — everything needs to be relearned.

Over these years, I've genuinely learned a lot. But honestly, most of the gains came from the products built by infra teams — their work is well-designed, clearly documented, and a pleasure to use.

As for the product code itself, a lot of it is a mess. Legacy baggage, stopgap solutions, all kinds of hacks... You might think big tech code would be elegant, but that's not necessarily the case. Under business pressure, if it runs, it ships — who has time to refactor?

This is probably the fate of all product development.

Writing all this, I'm not saying Meta is bad. Working here for several years has been a very important chapter of my career. I've learned things, broadened my horizons, and come to grips with some realities.

I just want to say — big tech isn't that magical.

It has great processes, but also the bureaucracy that processes bring. It has top-tier talent, but also people who coast. It has rigorous evaluations, but also the utilitarianism that evaluations breed. It has cutting-edge technology, but also messy product code.

Behind the halo, it's ordinary people doing ordinary work.

Only after the mystique fades can you see what you truly want.
